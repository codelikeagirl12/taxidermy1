What is taxidermy?

Taxidermy is the art of preparing, stuffing, and mounting animals for display or scientific study. This process typically involves placing the real skin of an animal over a man-made form to create a lifelike appearance, preserving the creature's likeness when it was alive.

While many of the animals on display in museums may appear lifelike, not all specimens are prepared this way. In fact, most are carefully preserved behind the scenes in the museum's collections using various methods. Some are immersed in ethanol, some are articulated as skeletons, and others are prepared as study skins. Study skins are a simplified form of taxidermy: the animal is skinned, stuffed, and allowed to dry, but they are not intended to look alive. Instead, they serve as valuable tools for scientific research and study.

Did u know ?

  • Many exhibition-goers frequently ask if the animals on display are real. The answer is affirmative - these creatures indeed appear genuine on the outside, even though they are no longer living. Typically, the only artificial components you'll find on the exterior of these display animals are replacement glass eyes and occasional touch-up paint.

  • Taxidermy, as an educational tool, serves to showcase and teach about the rich tapestry of life on Earth. Despite the prevalence of modern technology, many people maintain that having a lifelike, taxidermied animal in front of them has a more profound impact, creating a close and authentic connection to the world of wildlife.

Process of taxidermy

Scroll through the images on this digital story to
learn about the taxidermy process used to stuff a Brushtail
Possum for display.

1

After a Brushtail Possum is collected or donated to the Museum,
the skin is removed from the body and measurements are taken.

The measurements of the original flesh and muscle areas are drawn so artificial body parts can be created to replace the original to size. various fibres such as cotton are used.

2

3

A mould is taken of the possum's head using silicon and an
artificial head is casted using polyurethane. A wire is inserted into
the cast skull.

The body shape is made using coconut fibre and wood, and the cast head is attached to the body.

4

5

Cotton is used to secure the body to the head
support.

Wire being a flexible material functions like bones and ligaments acting like an internal support.

6

7

Clay is used to hold a replacement glass eyes, the skin is kept soft
using water, and the body is inserted into the original skin.

The body is stitched up following any further filling being
inserted, and the pins are used to hold the specimen in place as it dries.

Artists

Below, you'll find a selection of taxidermy artists who are pushing the boundaries, transforming taxidermy into a
rare and complex form of art.

Krysten Newby

Krysten has Obsessive Creative Disorder, driven by her love for
nature. Her unique career as an ethical taxidermist lets her share nature's
beauty up close. She supports wildlife conservation through charitable
donations, aiming to celebrate and protect the world's diverse wildlife.

Nichola Theakston

Theakston is a prominent contemporary sculptor in the UK, known for her work
in the animal genre. With a background in fine arts and a master's degree,
she creates ceramic and bronze sculptures. Her art is widely exhibited and
admired for its natural skill and deep connection to the subject.

Kate Clark

Brooklyn-based artist Kate Clark is meticulous in her efforts to
faithfully recreate the bodies of wild animals. However, she takes
a distinctive approach when sculpting their faces. In a unique twist, she
imbues her sculptures with human facial features, which she models
after live subjects visiting her studio.
